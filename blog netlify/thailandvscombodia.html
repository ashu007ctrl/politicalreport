Thailand–Cambodia War 2025

Published July 26, 2025

Reason Behind War

It all goes back decades—and even centuries. The Preah Vihear temple is the main reason behind this conflict, a jewel from ancient times that both Thailand and Cambodia claim as their own. You might remember a 1962 court ruling that largely settled the border, but on-the-ground realities don’t always match maps in dusty archives.

Fast-forward to late 2024: new surveys and patrols stirred national pride and political posturing. Thailand’s leaders accused Cambodia of creeping into a buffer zone, while Phnom Penh fired back, saying Thai troops were the real trespassers. Before you knew it, January 5, 2025, saw the first cannon blasts in years.

Army Comparison

Numbers can be dry, but here’s the guess:

  • Thailand fields about 100 M109A5 howitzers, 150 BMP-2 IFVs, and agile AH-1Z attack helicopters. They’ve been training across ASEAN and with UN missions, so they come battle-ready.
  • Cambodia counters with around 50 upgraded T-72 tanks, 40 BM-21 rocket launchers, plus Chinese-supplied drones and radars that help spot targets from afar.

In simple terms: Thailand has more firepower on paper, but Cambodia’s recent upgrades—and the way they’ve modernized logistics—means this won’t be a one-sided fight.

Thailand and the US - Old Friends

Thailand has leaned on the U.S. for decades—think Cobra Gold exercises and gear like F-16s and Black Hawk helicopters. In June 2025, Washington greenlit a $200 million package to sharpen Thailand’s border watch and cyber defenses. It’s clear America wants to keep its foothold here.

Cambodia and China: A Growing Partnership

Meanwhile, Cambodia has turned to Beijing—big roads, bridges, and now, military kit. They’ve got CH-4 drones buzzing overhead and PLA advisers tucked into their ranks. Plus, China’s clout in ASEAN shields Phnom Penh from the harshest criticism.

What’s next :

ASEAN’s scrambling to calm things down—late July 2025 talks aim for soldier pull-backs and joint patrols. But until Bangkok and Phnom Penh see real benefits in peace, villages near the border will stay on edge.

Will outside pressure—American warnings and Chinese nudges—tip the balance? Or will national pride keep both sides digging in? Either way, this clash is a reminder that old wounds don’t heal overnight.
